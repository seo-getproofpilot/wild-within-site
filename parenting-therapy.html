Parenting Therapy · Mesa, AZ

This is for you, the parent.

Therapy for the human raising the kids — not a class about how to raise them better.

You love your kids. You're doing your best. And you're running on empty.

Maybe you're snapping more than you want to. Maybe you're carrying guilt that follows you into every quiet moment. Maybe you're parenting in ways that look nothing like how you wanted to, and you can hear yourself sounding like the version of your own parent you swore you'd never become. Maybe a teenager just slammed a door, or a baby won't sleep, or you got divorced six months ago and you're still figuring out how to do bedtime alone.

This page is not for your kids. It's for you.

Parenting therapy is not a parenting class

I want to be clear up front about what this is and isn't.

This isn't a Love & Logic curriculum. It isn't family therapy with your kids in the room. It isn't a workshop on consequences and reward charts. There are great resources for that and they're not me.

Parenting therapy is a space for you to process what parenting is bringing up in you. The exhaustion. The grief. The unprocessed stuff from your own childhood that's quietly running the show. The version of yourself who shows up in your own kid's worst moment and behaves in a way you don't recognize. The shame that lives underneath all of it.

Parenting is one of the most spiritual, sacred, body-and-soul-rearranging experiences a human can have. And almost nobody talks about how hard it is to do it without losing yourself.

This is some of my favorite work

I'm a single mom of two beautiful, very active, neurodiverse boys. I've made every parenting mistake in the book. I've yelled when I meant to listen. I've apologized at midnight. I've sat in the car after dropping them off and cried because I was so depleted I didn't know how to stand up.

I coach parents from a place of grace, non-judgment, and full compassion — because I am one, and I know.

I'm trained in Positive Discipline, which gives us a healthy way of looking at child development, behavior, and challenges. I bring that into the room. But we spend most of our time on you, your story, your nervous system, and what you need so that the version of you who shows up at home is the parent you actually want to be.

Reasons parents come in

  • Parenting burnout — the kind no amount of "self-care" fixes
  • Mom guilt or dad guilt that won't quiet down
  • Anger you can't explain, especially anger at your own kids
  • Parenting through divorce, separation, or co-parenting hell
  • Blended family stress and stepparenting tension
  • Raising a teen who's pulling away
  • Parenting a neurodivergent child and feeling unequipped
  • Your own unhealed childhood showing up in your parenting
  • The isolation of doing this without enough support
  • Wanting to parent differently than you were parented and not knowing how

What it looks like

Parenting therapy is held the same way as my individual therapy. 50–60 minute sessions, weekly or every other week, in-person at our Mesa office or via secure telehealth across Arizona. $185 per session, out-of-network with superbills available.

You can come alone. You can come with a co-parent if that feels right. We'll talk about what you need.

FAQ

When should a parent see a therapist?

Whenever the version of you that shows up at home doesn't match the parent you want to be. You don't need a crisis. You don't need a "problem child." You just need to feel like something needs to shift.

Is this the same as parenting classes?

No. Parenting classes teach you techniques to manage your child's behavior. Parenting therapy is a space for you, the parent, to do your own work. Both can be useful — they're different things.

Is this for parents with "problem kids"?

This is for parents. Period. Your child doesn't need to be in crisis. Most parents I see have perfectly fine kids and just need a place to be a person.

Can both parents come?

Yes. We can do co-parent sessions if that's useful. That said, parenting therapy is a different thing from couples counseling — if the relationship is also struggling, we may want to do both.

How is this different from family therapy?

Family therapy includes the whole family system, often with kids in the room. Parenting therapy is one-on-one (or two-on-one) with you, the parent. The kids are not the focus. You are.

You're not failing. You're depleted.
